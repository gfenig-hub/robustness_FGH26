What do these figures show?

These figures display how subjects' conjectures about others' investments respond to the actual past investments of their group members.

Regression specification (main, 5 lags):

F(ĝ−i,t) = α + 5∑L=1βL · F(g−i,t−L) + εi,t

Where F(·) denotes the generalized mean of degree ρ, ĝ-i,t is subject i's conjecture about other group members' investments in period t, and g-i,t-L is the vector of actual investments by other members in round t-L.

The coefficients βL measure how strongly subjects update their beliefs based on what others did L rounds ago.

Note: Only the 4-window view uses a reduced 2-lag specification (β1, β2), as the narrow early windows (3–6, 7–10) do not contain enough observations to support 5 lags. The 2-window and 1-window views use the full 5-lag specification above.

Key dimensions:

  • Subject types: Homo Pecuniarius vs. Homo Behavioralis, or all subjects pooled
  • Treatments: LC (Low Complementarity) vs. HC (High Complementarity)
  • Time windows: Three partitions of the experiment — four windows (3-6, 7-10, 11-15, 16-20; 2-lag regression), two windows (6-15 and 16-20; 5-lag regression), or a single window (6-20; 5-lag regression)

Conjecture Variability Across Time Windows

Within-subject standard deviation of generalized mean of conjectures

What do these figures show?

For each subject and window, we compute the standard deviation of the generalized mean across that subject's calculator invocations (note that a subject can invoke the calculator several times in the same period, so a within-subject SD is defined even for a single period such as Practice). This metric captures within-subject conjecture variability: high values suggest the subject is still exploring or uncertain, low values indicate stabilized conjectures. Subjects with fewer than two calculator invocations in a window are excluded from that bar.

The Practice bar (period 0, shown in grey) is a pre-game benchmark. Significance brackets show paired t-tests. * p<0.1, ** p<0.05, *** p<0.01; n.s. = not significant.

Pecuniary Loss Index (PLI) Over Time

Mean PLI across time windows by type and treatment

What do these figures show?

The Pecuniary Loss Index (PLI) measures the percentage gap between a subject's actual payoff and the payoff they would have earned from the best response to any plausible combination of other group members' recent investments. A PLI of zero means the subject's investment can be fully rationalized as a monetary best response.

Type Stability: Classification Robustness

Is the type classification robust across different time periods?

What do these figures show?

We test whether the classification of subjects into Homo Pecuniarius (HP) and Homo Behavioralis (HB) is robust across different time periods. Both analyses use the same methodology (Ward's hierarchical clustering on PLI, separately by complementarity level).

Non-Overlapping Windows: Pick two disjoint windows A and B (end of A < start of B) and compare the classifications they produce. Tests whether types identified in one period generalize to another.

Overlapping Windows: Pick any window and compare its classification to the paper's full-experiment classification (rounds 2-20). Tests which rounds drive the paper's classification.
